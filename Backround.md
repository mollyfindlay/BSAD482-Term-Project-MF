## Backgrounder 
Newfoundland and Labrador is at a crossroads when it comes to the future of energy production. As they are a province with ample natural resources, they are the perfect candidate for both coastal wind turbine development and hydro-electric power development moving into the future. This is a possible time for this east coast province to become a pioneer when it comes to energy production and climate initiatives in the maritimes. This is a complex decision that must be sufficiency backed by reliable data, as there are many stakeholders involved in this decision. Renewable energy is a major component of Newfoundland and Labrador's wellbeing as “over 80 per cent of the province’s electricity is generated from renewable energy resources” (Government of Newfoundland and Labrador, 2021). While the province predominantly supplies energy using hydroelectric plants, there is a growing demand for alternatives that are more reliable and give off less emissions. 

There are many factors that make this decision difficult such as the differing benefits and drawbacks of each option. Both projects provide a positive impact compared to fossil fuel, but it is difficult to discern which would provide the most impact when it comes to meeting climate targets. A new hydroelectric project would require ample planning and funding from the NL Government. This method of renewable energy has brought high tensions in the past, as the high costs and possible delays worry many stakeholders. On the positive side, Newfoundland and Labrador primarily uses hydro energy and possesses already existing infrastructure that could be expanded upon in the future. Wind-technology is a rapidly expanding sector that has the potential to be a new frontier for achieving climate goals in Atlantic Canada. While offshore wind infrastructure offers a more reliable solution that uses less land capital, it would be a costly investment for the government of Newfoundland and Labrador to pursue. This new frontier for the island also comes with some uncertainty, as this emerging field may bring skepticism from stakeholders on if this is the correct choice for NL. 

There has been high tensions regarding energy production in the past. In 2012, a renewable hydroelectric plant was approved by the government of NL with a price tag of $7.4 billion. When the project finally finished in April of 2023, it had gone over budget by $6.1 billion with the final cost coming to $13.5 billion (Fitzpatrick, 2024). This caused an uproar within communities across the island, who now had to pay the price for this organizational mismanagement. Not only did the project finish years after the planned end date, but it cost NL taxpayers $190.4 million for the outstanding balance (Canada Broadcasting Corporation, 2023). Before the failure, this project was seen as a hope for Newfoundland and Labrador in their continuing strides towards 100% renewable energy and reduction of greenhouse emissions. This has now cast doubt in the minds of stakeholders when evaluating possible avenues to achieving climate targets.



## Key Stakeholders 
The key stakeholders in this process include Corporations such as  Newfoundland and Labrador Hydro and Nalcor who are the major players in energy production in NL, legislative entities such as the provincial and federal government, environmental organizations, offshore wind developers, as well as the people of Newfoundland and Labrador (especially those living off the coast and/or near possible hydroelectric properties), and any subject/structure that uses the energy provided by these developments.
